Time v. Num of Rides

The number of rides per hour tracks as expected, with steady growth through the day highlighted by bumps at morning and afternoon commute times along with another around the time of most people's lunch breaks.

Hourly Ride Count by Area

Click a label to remove it from the chart!
While the Financial District bikeshare stations receive the heaviest use at all times, South Park, Civic Center and a few other stations actually peak in rides earlier than the Financial District does. Although I'm unfamiliar with the composition of LA's different neighborhoods, this most likely is due to different purposes in visiting each place. This data is based on where trips end, so it seems as if people head to the Financial District at the end of the day.

Time v. Duration of Trip

According to the data, the average bike trip throughout the day hovers between 20-30 minutes. This number gets as high as 50-65 minutes between midnight and 3 am, most likely due to people going for longer joyrides for pleasure rather than using the bikes as a form of quick transportation to their destination.

Neighborhood-Station Count and Neighborhood-Ride Count Ratios

The number of stations relative to the number of rides per area is relatively proportional. Any minor differences are most likely made up by increasing the number of bikes at the location rather than always adding another station. However, this also makes managing bike locations even more important.

Commuting Statistics

Commute Counts

Seasonal Weekly
Surprisingly enough, the majority of rides are during the Fall rather than Spring or Summer. This may be because in the hot summer months commuters would rather not exert themselves while getting around and arrive to work sweaty, but it is more comfortable to do so during the Fall.
The vast majority of bikeshare commuters have a monthly pass. The Flex and Walkup users are most likely those who rode during commute times but aren't commuters, or those testing out the service for a short period of time. However, it is interesting to note that even among the aggregate data, monthly passes are still the vast majority of passes which is a sign that the service is being used regularly by most of its users. A very good sign for the future of bikeshare services in Los Angeles.
